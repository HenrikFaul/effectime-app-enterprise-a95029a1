# Objections and Underlying Pains

## Purpose
Use this file when writing sales scripts, demo flows, website copy, or content that needs to proactively address resistance. For each objection: understand the real fear behind it, craft the bridge response, identify the proof element needed, and select the right content format to address it pre-emptively.

---

## Objection 1 — "We manage fine with Excel / Google Sheets"

**Surface statement**: We don't need software; our spreadsheet works for us.

**Underlying fear**: Change is risky, disruptive, and may not be worth the hassle. The person saying this often knows the spreadsheet is fragile but feels embarrassed to admit the extent of the problem, or doesn't want to own a failed implementation project.

**What they don't say aloud**: The spreadsheet breaks every time someone goes on holiday at the same time. We've had payroll errors. Our managers don't update it. It lives on one person's laptop.

**Bridge response**:
"We hear this often. The question isn't whether your spreadsheet works — it's what happens when two managers are absent at once, or when you need to run a year-end attendance report in 20 minutes. Spreadsheets handle the normal case; Effectime handles the edge cases that are currently costing you 2–3 hours a week without you noticing."

**Proof element needed**: A before/after scenario showing time reclaimed — e.g., "Monthly attendance reconciliation: 3 hours → 12 minutes." A case where a spreadsheet failure cost real time or caused a compliance issue.

**Content formats**: Blog post ("Signs your Excel leave tracker is actually a liability"), LinkedIn post from founder, comparison page (Effectime vs. spreadsheets), website FAQ answer.

## Objection 2 — "We tried HR software before and nobody used it"

**Surface statement**: Implementation failed; adoption was low; it gathered dust.

**Underlying fear**: Wasted budget and political embarrassment. The buyer was burned before and doesn't want to own another failed rollout. There's also a hidden belief that the problem is people (won't adopt), not process.

**What they don't say aloud**: The previous tool was too complex, had poor mobile UX, or required IT involvement for every change. The rollout had no champion, no training, and no mandate.

**Bridge response**:
"That's the most common thing we hear from people who eventually become our best customers. The question is: was it the software, or was it how it was deployed? Effectime is built for self-service — teams can log in, see their leave balance, and submit a request in under 60 seconds. We also give you a structured 3-week onboarding that ends with your first full team live. We measure success by whether your managers can stop using WhatsApp for leave requests within 30 days."

**Proof element needed**: Onboarding timeline visualization; specific adoption metric (e.g., "85% of employees active within first 30 days"); testimonial from a buyer who switched from a failed incumbent tool.

**Content formats**: Demo sequence focused on UX simplicity; case study with adoption story; onboarding explainer page on website.

---

## Objection 3 — "It's too expensive for our size"

**Surface statement**: We're too small to justify the cost.

**Underlying fear**: ROI is unclear; the cost feels certain but the benefit feels abstract. Often this is a proxy for "I'm not convinced this solves a real enough problem."

**What they don't say aloud**: I'm not sure what I'd be paying for; I can't explain the value to my boss.

**Bridge response**:
"Let's make the math concrete. If your HR manager or office admin spends 4 hours a week on manual coordination tasks — leave requests, attendance tracking, month-end reconciliation — that's 200 hours a year. At a blended cost of €25/hour, that's €5,000 in labor you're already spending. Effectime typically costs a fraction of that at your size, and the time savings happen in the first month."

**Proof element needed**: ROI calculator (build it); cost-of-status-quo framing; pricing page that presents cost per employee per month (not total, which feels large).

**Content formats**: ROI calculator on website; "What manual workforce coordination really costs" blog post; pricing page with per-employee framing; sales proposal template with ROI section.

---

## Objection 4 — "It takes too long to implement"

**Surface statement**: We don't have bandwidth to set up a new system right now.

**Underlying fear**: An implementation project means months of IT involvement, configuration work, data migration, and training. They've seen ERP horror stories.

**What they don't say aloud**: I'm already overwhelmed; this feels like it will add to my plate before it removes from it.

**Bridge response**:
"Effectime is not an ERP project. Most teams are fully live in 2–3 weeks. Week 1: configure your org structure and leave policies (we guide you through it, 2 hours total). Week 2: invite your team. Week 3: your first full leave cycle runs through the system. We've done this with companies your size dozens of times. There's no IT dependency."

**Proof element needed**: Implementation timeline graphic (3 phases, ~3 weeks); customer quote about fast setup; "What happens after you sign" explainer content.

**Content formats**: Website onboarding page; sales deck slide; email follow-up after demo; short LinkedIn video from founder.

---

## Objection 5 — "We don't need that many features"

**Surface statement**: It seems over-engineered for what we need.

**Underlying fear**: Paying for things they won't use; overwhelming their team; the product is bloatware.

**What they don't say aloud**: I only need leave management; everything else sounds complicated.

**Bridge response**:
"You don't have to use all of it on day one. Most customers start with leave management and attendance — that's it. The other capabilities are there when you grow into them, or when you realize the coverage problem is actually the one causing the most pain. You configure only what's relevant to you. The system doesn't surface what you haven't turned on."

**Proof element needed**: Modular onboarding path; feature activation map (start simple, add later); customer quote about "we started with just leave and added coverage planning six months in."

**Content formats**: Website feature page with "start here" path; onboarding email sequence; demo script (show only the relevant module for each persona).

---

## Cross-Cutting Proof Elements Needed
- At least 3 customer quotes addressing specific objections
- One quantified before/after case study
- Implementation timeline visual
- ROI calculator (even simple version)
- "What we don't do" honest positioning page or FAQ

### Objection

- Use the Discovery Objections Cheat Sheet today.
- Try Briefpoint for Faster, More Accurate Discovery Responses
Discovery objections are part of the job, but the process around drafting and responding often takes more time than it should.
- Object specifically to the objectionable part and still answer the valid portion.
- Plaintiff objects to this interrogatory as vague, ambiguous, argumentative, overbroad, unduly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. [This is a classic general objection you should use sparingly. Boilerplate objections do not go over well with judges.].
- Plaintiff objects to this interrogatory because it requires the responding party to marshal all of its available proof or the proof the party intends to offer at trial. [But the evidence marshaling objection will only get you so far.].
- W. Pico Furniture v. Superior Court (1961): Often cited California Supreme Court case for the idea that objections such as compound questions or assuming facts not in evidence, more appropriate for oral examination, are not grounds for objecting to written interrogatories.
- Be clear in your objections or risk waving them.
- Use this objection to challenge questions not conforming to proper questioning techniques.

### Content

- Always be specific about the privilege invoked, and refrain from disclosing privileged content.
- Draft contention interrogatories and other interrogatories seeking specific responses narrowly.

### Need

- Plaintiff objects to this interrogatory because the only possible purpose of this request is to harass and cause hardship to the plaintiff and needlessly increase the cost of litigation to the plaintiff or her counsel.
- Plaintiff objects because this request calls for the disclosure of attorney work product prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial. Moreover, the defendant has failed to demonstrate a substantial need and the substantial equivalent of which the defendant would be unable to obtain by other means without due hardship.

### General Principles

- Protect privileged or protected information.
- Limit requests that seek documents beyond a reasonable scope.
- Account for the parties’ resources and practical burdens of compliance.
- Avoid delays caused by vague or overly broad requests.
- Broad or undefined references to communications or documents.
- Requests for electronically stored information without limits on time, format, or source.
- Vague demands for tangible things without explaining what categories are included.
