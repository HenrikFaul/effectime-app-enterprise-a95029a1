# Narrative House

## Purpose
The narrative house is the overarching story that holds all Effectime marketing together. Individual messages, features, and campaigns should each be traceable to a place in this narrative. When the story is consistent across channels, brand recall compounds. Use this document to align the team, brief creatives, and maintain narrative coherence as the product and market evolve.

---

## The Big Problem — What We're Really Solving

Growing companies are not small enterprises. They have moved past the size where everyone knows everything informally — but they haven't yet built the systems that make coordination automatic. The result is a specific, painful, daily reality:

Leave requests arrive through three different channels. Nobody is sure what was approved. A site is understaffed because two managers both said yes to different people without checking each other. The month-end attendance report is a 3-hour manual exercise that is still wrong. The sprint starts on Monday and one of the core developers is on holiday — nobody told the project manager.

This is not a technology problem. It is a systems problem. And the reason it persists is that the tools available to these companies are either too simple (Excel, WhatsApp) or too complex and expensive (Workday, SAP). The middle of the market — the 30-to-150-employee company — has been underserved.

**The big problem in one sentence**: Growing companies are running their workforce coordination on tools designed for either personal use or enterprise IT departments — and neither fits.

---

## The Villain — What We're Fighting Against

The villain is not a competitor product. The villain is **invisible coordination chaos** — the accumulated cost of scattered processes that nobody has added up:

- The HR manager who knows the spreadsheet is wrong but doesn't have time to fix it
- The operations lead who only finds out the team is understaffed the morning it happens
- The founder who is still personally looped into every leave approval because "the system" is actually just trusting their judgment
- The project manager whose sprint plan doesn't account for a key person being on holiday

The villain is not malicious — it's just what happens in the absence of a system. It's the default. Effectime's job is to replace the default.

**Do not make the villain the buyer's fault.** The narrative frames the villain as a structural gap — something that happens to smart, capable people who don't have the right tool. Blame the gap, not the person.

---

## The Hero — Who We're Writing For

The hero is the manager who decides to change the default. The HR generalist who says "this cannot keep running on my personal notebook." The operations director who is tired of being surprised. The founder who wants to build something that runs without their constant supervision.

The hero is not looking for software. They are looking for a system that makes their team run predictably — so they can focus on the work that matters, not the coordination overhead of the work.

**The hero's transformation**: From reactive and scattered → to visible, in control, and building something that scales.

---

## The Product Role — Effectime as Enabler, Not Star

Effectime is not the hero. The hero is the manager. Effectime is the system that makes the hero's transformation possible.

This is critical. Marketing that positions the product as the hero ("Effectime is amazing and here's all the things it can do") loses to marketing that positions the customer as the hero ("here's what becomes possible for you when coordination works").

**Effectime's role in the story**: The reliable system behind the team that runs smoothly. Not a revolution. Not a magic tool. A foundation — the operational infrastructure that growing companies should have been running on for years.

---

## Category Narrative vs. Product Narrative

**Category narrative**: "Workforce coordination for growing companies is broken. The tools don't fit. The processes don't scale. The hidden cost is enormous — most people just haven't added it up. There's a better way to run this."
*Use when*: Speaking to an audience that isn't yet product-aware; content marketing; thought leadership; founding-story contexts; PR and press.

**Product narrative**: "Effectime gives your team one place to manage leave, attendance, coverage, and resource visibility — so the coordination that was costing you hours becomes something the system handles."
*Use when*: Product pages; demos; direct comparison contexts; ads targeting solution-aware buyers.

The category narrative earns trust and creates demand. The product narrative captures that demand and converts it. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other.

---

## Narrative Application by Format

| Format | Narrative Entry Point | Arc Depth |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn post (founder) | The villain or transformation observation | Problem → implication (no full arc needed) |
| Website hero section | The villain + promise of transformation | 2 sentences: problem + outcome |
| Sales deck opener | "A day in the life" of the problem | Full arc (problem → villain → hero → product) |
| Case study | Hero's story in the customer's words | Full arc with real data |
| LinkedIn ad | The transformation moment or single contrast | One beat only |
| PR pitch | Category narrative — the systemic problem | Problem framing, Effectime as solution proof |
| Demo opening | The specific trigger event | Direct from the buyer's current situation |

---

## Narrative Quality Check
Before publishing any major piece of content, ask:
1. Does this piece have a clear villain (the problem)?
2. Does it position the buyer as the hero, not the product?
3. Does it show a credible transformation — not just a feature list?
4. Is the product's role clear as an enabler, not a savior?
5. Does the tone match the "experienced operational advisor" voice — not the SaaS marketer?

### Product

- Determine the customer issue solved by your product.

### Problem

- Consider the what and the how of the problems that they have, utilize your foundational research, and focus on the areas that you know you are best positioned to help with.
- Use the elements of a story: A hero with a problem meets a guide who gives the hero a plan and calls him or her to action, resulting in either success or failure.
- Make the problem in your story specific to a pain point your customers face.

### General Principles

- Facts inform; stories transform. Square footage and specs matter, but stories make buyers care—and caring drives action.
- Trust and authenticity: Client success stories and behind-the-scenes moments humanize your brand and increase referrals.
- Decide the message first. Choose the takeaway: confidence, simplicity, community, or potential.
- Make clients the hero; be the guide. We position clients as protagonists overcoming obstacles; we provide the plan, tools, and clarity.
- Use human language. Skip corporate-speak and legalese. Write like you talk during a showing.
- Add honest conflict. Tight timelines, bidding wars, inspection surprises—show how you navigated them.
- The hero: your client (not us). We picture a specific person and life stage so the copy speaks to a real human, not a demographic.
- External: “Need more space,” “Tight timeline,” “Equity unclear.”.
