# Objection Handling Master Prompt

## Purpose
Comprehensive objection handling for Effectime sales conversations. For each objection: psychological root, reframe approach, bridge phrase, evidence to deploy.

---

## Objection 1: "We manage fine with Excel / Google Sheets"

**Psychological root:** Loss aversion. They've invested in their current system (even if it's just a habit). They fear the change will cause more disruption than it solves.

**Reframe:** Don't argue that Excel is bad. Acknowledge it works — then show what it costs as the company grows.

**Bridge:** "I hear this often from companies your size — and Excel absolutely works for a while. The question is: when does it start to cost more than it saves?"

**Evidence:** Use the process comparison from `messaging/feature_to_outcome_translation.md`. Walk through 2 scenarios: leave request and coverage check. Show the steps.

**What not to say:** "Excel is outdated." "Spreadsheets are unreliable." (Defensive; makes the prospect feel wrong.)

---

## Objection 2: "We tried HR software before and nobody used it"

**Psychological root:** Experience-based skepticism. They've been burned. The fear is wasted time and money on adoption.

**Reframe:** Acknowledge the experience. Then differentiate: why do tools fail? Usually: too complex, too many features, forced on people rather than chosen by them.

**Bridge:** "That's exactly why we built Effectime the way we did. The #1 reason HR tools fail is that they're too complex for everyday use. We designed for the person who just needs to approve a leave request — not for an HR director."

**Evidence:** "The average Effectime workspace has first team member onboarded within 48 hours of signup. That's not typical."

---

## Objection 3: "It's too expensive for our size"

**Psychological root:** Price anchoring. They're comparing to zero (spreadsheets cost nothing) or to other tools. May also be a proxy for "I'm not sure the ROI is there."

**Reframe:** Reframe the comparison. What is the current cost, in hours?

**Bridge:** "At [price], let's see what the comparison looks like. If your HR manager spends 4 hours per week on manual coordination — that's [cost] per year at their hourly rate. Is [price] worth recovering those 4 hours?"

**Evidence:** Customer outcome data on time saved.

---

## Objection 4: "Not the right time / we have other priorities"

**Psychological root:** Urgency mismatch. The pain isn't acute enough to justify attention. Or they're genuinely overwhelmed.

**Reframe:** Respect the timing. Then plant a stake.

**Bridge:** "That makes total sense — this isn't an emergency decision. Can I ask: what would need to change at your company for this to become a priority? [Listen.] Got it. I'll check back in [timeframe]. In the meantime — if you want to try the free trial without any commitment, it's there."

**What not to do:** Push harder. Create fake urgency ("Offer ends Friday").

---

## Objection 5: "We already have [competitor]"

**Psychological root:** Commitment to an existing investment. Switching cost aversion.

**Reframe:** Don't attack the competitor. Ask about what's working and what isn't.

**Bridge:** "That's great — [competitor] does [X] well. What's one thing you wish it did that it doesn't?"

**If they have a real gap Effectime fills:** "That's actually something we handle specifically. Would it make sense to run a trial alongside what you have, just to compare?"

---

## Objection 6: "We're too small for this"

**Psychological root:** Underestimating their own operational complexity. Or genuinely too early.

**Reframe:** Honest answer. If they're truly < 15 people with no complexity, acknowledge it.

**Bridge:** "Honestly, if you're under 15 people with one location and no coverage requirements — you might not need us yet. But if you've got multiple sites, specific skill requirements, or a growing team — that's exactly when this starts saving time."

---

## Objection 7: "Needs IT / management approval"

**Psychological root:** Bureaucratic friction. Not the decision-maker in front of you.

**Reframe:** Help them build the case for internal approval.

**Bridge:** "Totally understandable. What would help me is: what does [decision-maker] care most about — cost, security, integration, or something else? I can put together a one-pager that addresses exactly that."
